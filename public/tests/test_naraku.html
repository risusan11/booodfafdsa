Part 3: Reading (15 questions)

Passage A

For decades, historians assumed that early civilizational development followed a linear trajectory from small cooperative groups to large hierarchical states. Recent archaeological discoveries, however, challenge this narrative. Excavations across multiple continents reveal evidence of ancient societies that alternated between hierarchical and egalitarian structures depending on seasonal, economic, or ritual circumstances. These findings undermine the simplistic assumption that hierarchy is the inevitable outcome of social complexity. Instead, they suggest that prehistoric communities possessed political adaptability far exceeding that of many modern nations. Rather than being passive subjects of environmental pressures, these societies actively reinvented their governance structures in response to shifting collective needs.

Passage B

As artificial intelligence systems exhibit increasingly autonomous decision-making behaviors, philosophers and legal theorists are reconsidering foundational assumptions about moral agency. Traditional frameworks treat AI as passive instruments lacking intentions. Yet emerging algorithmic architectures—capable of generating self-modifying heuristics—blur boundaries between programmed response and genuine deliberation. This raises a difficult question: If an AI system evaluates moral trade-offs without explicit instruction, does accountability lie with its creator, its operator, or the system itself? Some scholars argue that responsibility must be distributed across entire socio-technical networks rather than assigned to a single agent. Others claim that attributing moral agency to AI risks eroding human responsibility altogether.

Passage C

Climate science faces extraordinary challenges due to the nonlinear nature of environmental interactions. Minor fluctuations in ocean salinity, for instance, can amplify atmospheric conditions thousands of kilometers away. Such feedback loops complicate predictive models, which must integrate data points spanning centuries and multiple ecosystems. Furthermore, climate data collection remains uneven. Regions with advanced monitoring technologies produce robust datasets, while politically unstable or economically disadvantaged areas lack long-term records. This disparity introduces biases into global models, making some projections more reliable than others. Scientists therefore emphasize expanding cross-national collaboration to strengthen predictive accuracy.
